48 /**
49  * @ingroup thermoprops
50  *
51  * Class DebyeHuckel represents a dilute liquid electrolyte phase which
52  * obeys the Debye Huckel formulation for nonideality.
53  *
54  * The concentrations of the ionic species are assumed to obey the electroneutrality
55  * condition.
56  *
57  * <HR>
58  * <H2> Specification of Species Standard State Properties </H2>
59  * <HR>
60  *
61  * The standard states are on the unit molality basis. Therefore, in the
62  * documentation below, the normal \f$ o \f$ superscript is replaced with
63  * the \f$ \triangle \f$ symbol. The reference state symbol is now
64  * \f$ \triangle, ref \f$.
65  *
66  * It is assumed that the reference state thermodynamics may be
67  * obtained by a pointer to a populated species thermodynamic property
68  * manager class (see ThermoPhase::m_spthermo). How to relate pressure
69  * changes to the reference state thermodynamics is resolved at this level.
70  *
71  * For an incompressible,
72  * stoichiometric substance, the molar internal energy is
73  * independent of pressure. Since the thermodynamic properties
74  * are specified by giving the standard-state enthalpy, the
75  * term \f$ P_0 \hat v\f$ is subtracted from the specified molar
76  * enthalpy to compute the molar internal energy. The entropy is
77  * assumed to be independent of the pressure.
78  *
79  * The enthalpy function is given by the following relation.
80  *
81  * \f[
82  * h^\triangle_k(T,P) = h^{\triangle,ref}_k(T)
83  * + \tilde v \left( P - P_{ref} \right)
84  * \f]
85  *
86  * For an incompressible,
87  * stoichiometric substance, the molar internal energy is
88  * independent of pressure. Since the thermodynamic properties
89  * are specified by giving the standard-state enthalpy, the
90  * term \f$ P_{ref} \tilde v\f$ is subtracted from the specified reference molar
91  * enthalpy to compute the molar internal energy.
92  *
93  * \f[
94  * u^\triangle_k(T,P) = h^{\triangle,ref}_k(T) - P_{ref} \tilde v
95  * \f]
96  *
97  * The standard state heat capacity and entropy are independent
98  * of pressure. The standard state Gibbs free energy is obtained
99  * from the enthalpy and entropy functions.
100  *
101  * The vector Phase::m_speciesSize[] is used to hold the
102  * base values of species sizes. These are defined as the
103  * molar volumes of species at infinite dilution at 300 K and 1 atm
104  * of water. m_speciesSize are calculated during the initialization of the
105  * DebyeHuckel object and are then not touched.
106  *
107  * The current model assumes that an incompressible molar volume for
108  * all solutes. The molar volume for the water solvent, however,
109  * is obtained from a pure water equation of state, waterSS.
110  * Therefore, the water standard state varies with both T and P.
111  * It is an error to request standard state water properties at a T and P
112  * where the water phase is not a stable phase, i.e., beyond its
113  * spinodal curve.
114  *
115  * <HR>
116  * <H2> Specification of Solution Thermodynamic Properties </H2>
117  * <HR>
118  *
119  * Chemical potentials
120  * of the solutes, \f$ \mu_k \f$, and the solvent, \f$ \mu_o \f$, which are based
121  * on the molality form, have the following general format:
122  *
123  * \f[
124  * \mu_k = \mu^{\triangle}_k(T,P) + R T ln(\gamma_k^{\triangle} \frac{m_k}{m^\triangle})
125  * \f]
126  * \f[
127  * \mu_o = \mu^o_o(T,P) + RT ln(a_o)
128  * \f]
129  *
130  * where \f$ \gamma_k^{\triangle} \f$ is the molality based activity coefficient for species
131  * \f$k\f$.
132  *
133  * Individual activity coefficients of ions can not be independently measured. Instead,
134  * only binary pairs forming electroneutral solutions can be measured.
135  *
136  * <H3> Ionic Strength </H3>
137  *
138  * Most of the parameterizations within the model use the ionic strength
139  * as a key variable. The ionic strength, \f$ I\f$ is defined as follows
140  *
141  * \f[
142  * I = \frac{1}{2} \sum_k{m_k z_k^2}
143  * \f]
144  *
145  * \f$ m_k \f$ is the molality of the kth species. \f$ z_k \f$ is the charge
146  * of the kth species. Note, the ionic strength is a defined units quantity.
147  * The molality has defined units of gmol kg-1, and therefore the ionic
148  * strength has units of sqrt( gmol kg-1).
149  *
150  * In some instances, from some authors, a different
151  * formulation is used for the ionic strength in the equations below. The different
152  * formulation is due to the possibility of the existence of weak acids and how
153  * association wrt to the weak acid equilibrium relation affects the calculation
154  * of the activity coefficients via the assumed value of the ionic strength.
155  *
156  * If we are to assume that the association reaction doesn't have an effect
157  * on the ionic strength, then we will want to consider the associated weak
158  * acid as in effect being fully dissociated, when we calculate an effective
159  * value for the ionic strength. We will call this calculated value, the
160  * stoichiometric ionic strength, \f$ I_s \f$, putting a subscript s to denote
161  * it from the more straightforward calculation of \f$ I \f$.
162  *
163  * \f[
164  * I_s = \frac{1}{2} \sum_k{m_k^s z_k^2}
165  * \f]
166  *
167  * Here, \f$ m_k^s \f$ is the value of the molalities calculated assuming that
168  * all weak acid-base pairs are in their fully dissociated states. This calculation may
169  * be simplified by considering that the weakly associated acid may be made up of two
170  * charged species, k1 and k2, each with their own charges, obeying the following relationship:
171  *
172  * \f[
173  * z_k = z_{k1} + z_{k2}
174  * \f]
175  * Then, we may only need to specify one charge value, say, \f$ z_{k1}\f$,
176  * the cation charge number,
177  * in order to get both numbers, since we have already specified \f$ z_k \f$
178  * in the definition of original species.
179  * Then, the stoichiometric ionic strength may be calculated via the following formula.
180  *
181  * \f[
182  * I_s = \frac{1}{2} \left(\sum_{k,ions}{m_k z_k^2}+
183  * \sum_{k,weak_assoc}(m_k z_{k1}^2 + m_k z_{k2}^2) \right)
184  * \f]
185  *
186  * The specification of which species are weakly associated acids is made in the input
187  * file via the
188  * <TT> stoichIsMods </TT> XML block, where the charge for k1 is also specified.
189  * An example is given below:
190  *
191  * @code
192  * <stoichIsMods>
193  * NaCl(aq):-1.0
194  * </stoichIsMods>
195  * @endcode
196  *
197  * Because we need the concept of a weakly associated acid in order to calculated
198  * \f$ I_s \f$ we need to
199  * catalog all species in the phase. This is done using the following categories:
200  *
201  * - <B>cEST_solvent</B> Solvent species (neutral)
202  * - <B>cEST_chargedSpecies</B> Charged species (charged)
203  * - <B>cEST_weakAcidAssociated</B> Species which can break apart into charged species.
204  * It may or may not be charged. These may or
205  * may not be be included in the
206  * species solution vector.
207  * - <B>cEST_strongAcidAssociated</B> Species which always breaks apart into charged species.
208  * It may or may not be charged. Normally, these aren't included
209  * in the speciation vector.
210  * - <B>cEST_polarNeutral </B> Polar neutral species
211  * - <B>cEST_nonpolarNeutral</B> Non polar neutral species
212  *
213  * Polar and non-polar neutral species are differentiated, because some additions
214  * to the activity
215  * coefficient expressions distinguish between these two types of solutes. This is the so-called
216  * salt-out effect.
217  *
218  * The type of species is specified in the <TT>electrolyteSpeciesType</TT> XML block.
219  * Note, this is not
220  * considered a part of the specification of the standard state for the species,
221  * at this time. Therefore,
222  * this information is put under the <TT>activityCoefficient</TT> XML block. An example
223  * is given below
224  *
225  * @code
226  * <electrolyteSpeciesType>
227  * H2L(L):solvent
228  * H+:chargedSpecies
229  * NaOH(aq):weakAcidAssociated
230  * NaCl(aq):strongAcidAssociated
231  * NH3(aq):polarNeutral
232  * O2(aq):nonpolarNeutral
233  * </electrolyteSpeciesType>
234  * @endcode
235  *
236  * Much of the species electrolyte type information is inferred from other information in the
237  * input file. For example, as species which is charged is given the "chargedSpecies" default
238  * category. A neutral solute species is put into the "nonpolarNeutral" category by default.
239  *
240  * The specification of solute activity coefficients depends on the model
241  * assumed for the Debye-Huckel term. The model is set by the
242  * internal parameter #m_formDH. We will now describe each category in its own section.
243  *
244  * <H3> Debye-Huckel Dilute Limit </H3>
245  *
246  * DHFORM_DILUTE_LIMIT = 0
247  *
248  * This form assumes a dilute limit to DH, and is mainly for informational purposes:
249  * \f[
250  * \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle) = - z_k^2 A_{Debye} \sqrt{I}
251  * \f]
252  * where \f$ I\f$ is the ionic strength
253  * \f[
254  * I = \frac{1}{2} \sum_k{m_k z_k^2}
255  * \f]
256  *
257  * The activity for the solvent water,\f$ a_o \f$, is not independent and must be
258  * determined from the Gibbs-Duhem relation.
259  *
260  * \f[
261  * \ln(a_o) = \frac{X_o - 1.0}{X_o} + \frac{ 2 A_{Debye} \tilde{M}_o}{3} (I)^{3/2}
262  * \f]
263  *
264  * <H3> Bdot Formulation </H3>
265  *
266  * DHFORM_BDOT_AK = 1
267  *
268  * This form assumes Bethke's format for the Debye Huckel activity coefficient:
269  *
270  * \f[
271  * \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle) = -z_k^2 \frac{A_{Debye} \sqrt{I}}{ 1 + B_{Debye} a_k \sqrt{I}}
272  * + \log(10) B^{dot}_k I
273  * \f]
274  *
275  * Note, this particular form where \f$ a_k \f$ can differ in multielectrolyte
276  * solutions has problems with respect to a Gibbs-Duhem analysis. However,
277  * we include it here because there is a lot of data fit to it.
278  *
279  * The activity for the solvent water,\f$ a_o \f$, is not independent and must be
280  * determined from the Gibbs-Duhem relation. Here, we use:
281  *
282  * \f[
283  * \ln(a_o) = \frac{X_o - 1.0}{X_o}
284  * + \frac{ 2 A_{Debye} \tilde{M}_o}{3} (I)^{1/2}
285  * \left[ \sum_k{\frac{1}{2} m_k z_k^2 \sigma( B_{Debye} a_k \sqrt{I} ) } \right]
286  * - \frac{\log(10)}{2} \tilde{M}_o I \sum_k{ B^{dot}_k m_k}
287  * \f]
288  * where
289  * \f[
290  * \sigma (y) = \frac{3}{y^3} \left[ (1+y) - 2 \ln(1 + y) - \frac{1}{1+y} \right]
291  * \f]
292  *
293  * Additionally, Helgeson's formulation for the water activity is offered as an
294  * alternative.
295  *
296  * <H3> Bdot Formulation with Uniform Size Parameter in the Denominator </H3>
297  *
298  * DHFORM_BDOT_AUNIFORM = 2
299  *
300  * This form assumes Bethke's format for the Debye-Huckel activity coefficient
301  *
302  * \f[
303  * \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle) = -z_k^2 \frac{A_{Debye} \sqrt{I}}{ 1 + B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I}}
304  * + \log(10) B^{dot}_k I
305  * \f]
306  *
307  * The value of a is determined at the beginning of the calculation, and not changed.
308  *
309  * \f[
310  * \ln(a_o) = \frac{X_o - 1.0}{X_o}
311  * + \frac{ 2 A_{Debye} \tilde{M}_o}{3} (I)^{3/2} \sigma( B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I} )
312  * - \frac{\log(10)}{2} \tilde{M}_o I \sum_k{ B^{dot}_k m_k}
313  * \f]
314  *
315  * <H3> Beta_IJ formulation </H3>
316  *
317  * DHFORM_BETAIJ = 3
318  *
319  * This form assumes a linear expansion in a virial coefficient form.
320  * It is used extensively in the book by Newmann, "Electrochemistry Systems",
321  * and is the beginning of more complex treatments for stronger electrolytes,
322  * fom Pitzer and from Harvey, Moller, and Weire.
323  *
324  * \f[
325  * \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle) = -z_k^2 \frac{A_{Debye} \sqrt{I}}{ 1 + B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I}}
326  * + 2 \sum_j \beta_{j,k} m_j
327  * \f]
328  *
329  * In the current treatment the binary interaction coefficients, \f$ \beta_{j,k}\f$, are
330  * independent of temperature and pressure.
331  *
332  * \f[
333  * \ln(a_o) = \frac{X_o - 1.0}{X_o}
334  * + \frac{ 2 A_{Debye} \tilde{M}_o}{3} (I)^{3/2} \sigma( B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I} )
335  * - \tilde{M}_o \sum_j \sum_k \beta_{j,k} m_j m_k
336  * \f]
337  *
338  * In this formulation the ionic radius, \f$ a \f$, is a constant. This must be supplied to the
339  * model, in an <DFN> ionicRadius </DFN> XML block.
340  *
341  * The \f$ \beta_{j,k} \f$ parameters are binary interaction parameters. They are supplied to
342  * the object in an <TT> DHBetaMatrix </TT> XML block. There are in principle \f$ N (N-1) /2 \f$
343  * different, symmetric interaction parameters, where \f$ N \f$ are the number of solute species in the
344  * mechanism.
345  * An example is given below.
346  *
347  * An example <TT> activityCoefficients </TT> XML block for this formulation is supplied below
348  *
349  * @code
350  * <activityCoefficients model="Beta_ij">
351  * <!-- A_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol) -->
352  * <A_Debye> 1.172576 </A_Debye>
353  * <!-- B_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol)/m -->
354  * <B_Debye> 3.28640E9 </B_Debye>
355  * <ionicRadius default="3.042843" units="Angstroms">
356  * </ionicRadius>
357  * <DHBetaMatrix>
358  * H+:Cl-:0.27
359  * Na+:Cl-:0.15
360  * Na+:OH-:0.06
361  * </DHBetaMatrix>
362  * <stoichIsMods>
363  * NaCl(aq):-1.0
364  * </stoichIsMods>
365  * <electrolyteSpeciesType>
366  * H+:chargedSpecies
367  * NaCl(aq):weakAcidAssociated
368  * </electrolyteSpeciesType>
369  * </activityCoefficients>
370  * @endcode
371  *
372  * <H3> Pitzer Beta_IJ formulation </H3>
373  *
374  * DHFORM_PITZER_BETAIJ = 4
375  *
376  * This form assumes an activity coefficient formulation consistent
377  * with a truncated form of Pitzer's formulation. Pitzer's formulation is equivalent
378  * to the formulations above in the dilute limit, where rigorous theory may be applied.
379  *
380  * \f[
381  * \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle) = -z_k^2 \frac{A_{Debye}}{3} \frac{\sqrt{I}}{ 1 + B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I}}
382  * -2 z_k^2 \frac{A_{Debye}}{3} \frac{\ln(1 + B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I})}{ B_{Debye} a}
383  * + 2 \sum_j \beta_{j,k} m_j
384  * \f]
385  *
386  *
387  * \f[
388  * \ln(a_o) = \frac{X_o - 1.0}{X_o}
389  * + \frac{ 2 A_{Debye} \tilde{M}_o}{3} \frac{(I)^{3/2} }{1 + B_{Debye} a \sqrt{I} }
390  * - \tilde{M}_o \sum_j \sum_k \beta_{j,k} m_j m_k
391  * \f]
392  *
393  * <H3> Specification of the Debye Huckel Constants </H3>
394  *
395  * In the equations above, the formulas for \f$ A_{Debye} \f$ and \f$ B_{Debye} \f$
396  * are needed. The DebyeHuckel object uses two methods for specifying these quantities.
397  * The default method is to assume that \f$ A_{Debye} \f$ is a constant, given
398  * in the initialization process, and stored in the
399  * member double, m_A_Debye. Optionally, a full water treatment may be employed that makes
400  * \f$ A_{Debye} \f$ a full function of <I>T</I> and <I>P</I>.
401  *
402  * \f[
403  * A_{Debye} = \frac{F e B_{Debye}}{8 \pi \epsilon R T} {\left( C_o \tilde{M}_o \right)}^{1/2}
404  * \f]
405  * where
406  *
407  * \f[
408  * B_{Debye} = \frac{F} {{(\frac{\epsilon R T}{2})}^{1/2}}
409  * \f]
410  * Therefore:
411  * \f[
412  * A_{Debye} = \frac{1}{8 \pi}
413  * {\left(\frac{2 N_a \rho_o}{1000}\right)}^{1/2}
414  * {\left(\frac{N_a e^2}{\epsilon R T }\right)}^{3/2}
415  * \f]
416  *
417  * where
418  * - \f$ N_a \f$ is Avogadro's number
419  * - \f$ \rho_w \f$ is the density of water
420  * - \f$ e \f$ is the electronic charge
421  * - \f$ \epsilon = K \epsilon_o \f$ is the permittivity of water
422  * - \f$ K \f$ is the dielectric constant of water
423  * - \f$ \epsilon_o \f$ is the permittivity of free space
424  * - \f$ \rho_o \f$ is the density of the solvent in its standard state.
425  *
426  * Nominal value at 298 K and 1 atm = 1.172576 (kg/gmol)<SUP>1/2</SUP> based on:
427  * - \f$ \epsilon / \epsilon_0 \f$ = 78.54 (water at 25C)
428  * - T = 298.15 K
429  * - B_Debye = 3.28640E9 (kg/gmol)<SUP>1/2</SUP> m<SUP>-1</SUP>
430  *
431  * An example of a fixed value implementation is given below.
432  * @code
433  * <activityCoefficients model="Beta_ij">
434  * <!-- A_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol) -->
435  * <A_Debye> 1.172576 </A_Debye>
436  * <!-- B_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol)/m -->
437  * <B_Debye> 3.28640E9 </B_Debye>
438  * </activityCoefficients>
439  * @endcode
440  *
441  * An example of a variable value implementation is given below.
442  *
443  * @code
444  * <activityCoefficients model="Beta_ij">
445  * <A_Debye model="water" />
446  * <!-- B_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol)/m -->
447  * <B_Debye> 3.28640E9 </B_Debye>
448  * </activityCoefficients>
449  * @endcode
450  *
451  * Currently, \f$ B_{Debye} \f$ is a constant in the model, specified either by a default
452  * water value, or through the input file. This may have to be looked at, in the future.
453  *
454  * <HR>
455  * <H2> %Application within Kinetics Managers </H2>
456  * <HR>
457  *
458  * For the time being, we have set the standard concentration for all species in
459  * this phase equal to the default concentration of the solvent at 298 K and 1 atm.
460  * This means that the
461  * kinetics operator essentially works on an activities basis, with units specified
462  * as if it were on a concentration basis.
463  *
464  * For example, a bulk-phase binary reaction between liquid species j and k, producing
465  * a new liquid species l would have the
466  * following equation for its rate of progress variable, \f$ R^1 \f$, which has
467  * units of kmol m-3 s-1.
468  *
469  * \f[
470  * R^1 = k^1 C_j^a C_k^a = k^1 (C_o a_j) (C_o a_k)
471  * \f]
472  * where
473  * \f[
474  * C_j^a = C_o a_j \quad and \quad C_k^a = C_o a_k
475  * \f]
476  *
477  * \f$ C_j^a \f$ is the activity concentration of species j, and
478  * \f$ C_k^a \f$ is the activity concentration of species k. \f$ C_o \f$
479  * is the concentration of water at 298 K and 1 atm. \f$ a_j \f$ is
480  * the activity of species j at the current temperature and pressure
481  * and concentration of the liquid phase. \f$k^1 \f$ has units of m3 kmol-1 s-1.
482  *
483  * The reverse rate constant can then be obtained from the law of microscopic reversibility
484  * and the equilibrium expression for the system.
485  *
486  * \f[
487  * \frac{a_j a_k}{ a_l} = K^{o,1} = \exp(\frac{\mu^o_l - \mu^o_j - \mu^o_k}{R T} )
488  * \f]
489  *
490  * \f$ K^{o,1} \f$ is the dimensionless form of the equilibrium constant.
491  *
492  * \f[
493  * R^{-1} = k^{-1} C_l^a = k^{-1} (C_o a_l)
494  * \f]
495  *
496  * where
497  *
498  * \f[
499  * k^{-1} = k^1 K^{o,1} C_o
500  * \f]
501  *
502  * \f$k^{-1} \f$ has units of s-1.
503  *
504  * Note, this treatment may be modified in the future, as events dictate.
505  *
506  * <HR>
507  * <H2> Instantiation of the Class </H2>
508  * <HR>
509  *
510  * The constructor for this phase is NOT located in the default ThermoFactory
511  * for %Cantera. However, a new DebyeHuckel object may be created by
512  * the following code snippets:
513  *
514  * @code
515  * DebyeHuckel *DH = new DebyeHuckel("DH_NaCl.xml", "NaCl_electrolyte");
516  * @endcode
517  *
518  * or
519  *
520  * @code
521  * XML_Node *xm = get_XML_NameID("phase", "DH_NaCl.xml#NaCl_electrolyte", 0);
522  * DebyeHuckel *dh = new DebyeHuckel(*xm);
523  * @endcode
524  *
525  * or by the following call to importPhase():
526  *
527  * @code
