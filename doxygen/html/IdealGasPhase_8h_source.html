18 //! Class IdealGasPhase represents low-density gases that obey the ideal gas
19 //! equation of state.
20 /*!
21  *
22  * IdealGasPhase derives from class ThermoPhase, and overloads the virtual
23  * methods defined there with ones that use expressions appropriate for ideal
24  * gas mixtures.
25  *
26  * The independent unknowns are density, mass fraction, and temperature. the
27  * #setPressure() function will calculate the density consistent with the
28  * current mass fraction vector and temperature and the desired pressure, and
29  * then set the density.
30  *
31  * ## Specification of Species Standard State Properties
32  *
33  * It is assumed that the reference state thermodynamics may be obtained by a
34  * pointer to a populated species thermodynamic property manager class in the
35  * base class, ThermoPhase::m_spthermo (see the base class \link
36  * Cantera::MultiSpeciesThermo MultiSpeciesThermo \endlink for a description of
37  * the specification of reference state species thermodynamics functions). The
38  * reference state, where the pressure is fixed at a single pressure, is a key
39  * species property calculation for the Ideal Gas Equation of state.
40  *
41  * This class is optimized for speed of execution. All calls to thermodynamic
42  * functions first call internal routines (aka #enthalpy_RT_ref()) which return
43  * references the reference state thermodynamics functions. Within these
44  * internal reference state functions, the function #_updateThermo() is called,
45  * that first checks to see whether the temperature has changed. If it has, it
46  * updates the internal reference state thermo functions by calling the
47  * MultiSpeciesThermo object.
48  *
49  * Functions for the calculation of standard state properties for species at
50  * arbitrary pressure are provided in IdealGasPhase. However, they are all
51  * derived from their reference state counterparts.
52  *
53  * The standard state enthalpy is independent of pressure:
54  *
55  * \f[
56  * h^o_k(T,P) = h^{ref}_k(T)
57  * \f]
58  *
59  * The standard state constant-pressure heat capacity is independent of pressure:
60  *
61  * \f[
62  * Cp^o_k(T,P) = Cp^{ref}_k(T)
63  * \f]
64  *
65  * The standard state entropy depends in the following fashion on pressure:
66  *
67  * \f[
68  * S^o_k(T,P) = S^{ref}_k(T) - R \ln(\frac{P}{P_{ref}})
69  * \f]
70  * The standard state Gibbs free energy is obtained from the enthalpy and entropy
71  * functions:
72  *
73  * \f[
74  * \mu^o_k(T,P) = h^o_k(T,P) - S^o_k(T,P) T
75  * \f]
76  *
77  * \f[
78  * \mu^o_k(T,P) = \mu^{ref}_k(T) + R T \ln( \frac{P}{P_{ref}})
79  * \f]
80  *
81  * where
82  * \f[
83  * \mu^{ref}_k(T) = h^{ref}_k(T) - T S^{ref}_k(T)
84  * \f]
85  *
86  * The standard state internal energy is obtained from the enthalpy function also
87  *
88  * \f[
89  * u^o_k(T,P) = h^o_k(T) - R T
90  * \f]
91  *
92  * The molar volume of a species is given by the ideal gas law
93  *
94  * \f[
95  * V^o_k(T,P) = \frac{R T}{P}
96  * \f]
97  *
98  * where R is the molar gas constant. For a complete list of physical constants
99  * used within %Cantera, see \ref physConstants .
100  *
101  * ## Specification of Solution Thermodynamic Properties
102  *
103  * The activity of a species defined in the phase is given by the ideal gas law:
104  * \f[
105  * a_k = X_k
106  * \f]
107  * where \f$ X_k \f$ is the mole fraction of species *k*. The chemical potential
108  * for species *k* is equal to
109  *
110  * \f[
111  * \mu_k(T,P) = \mu^o_k(T, P) + R T \log(X_k)
112  * \f]
113  *
114  * In terms of the reference state, the above can be rewritten
115  *
116  * \f[
117  * \mu_k(T,P) = \mu^{ref}_k(T, P) + R T \log(\frac{P X_k}{P_{ref}})
118  * \f]
119  *
120  * The partial molar entropy for species *k* is given by the following relation,
121  *
122  * \f[
123  * \tilde{s}_k(T,P) = s^o_k(T,P) - R \log(X_k) = s^{ref}_k(T) - R \log(\frac{P X_k}{P_{ref}})
124  * \f]
125  *
126  * The partial molar enthalpy for species *k* is
127  *
128  * \f[
129  * \tilde{h}_k(T,P) = h^o_k(T,P) = h^{ref}_k(T)
130  * \f]
131  *
132  * The partial molar Internal Energy for species *k* is
133  *
134  * \f[
135  * \tilde{u}_k(T,P) = u^o_k(T,P) = u^{ref}_k(T)
136  * \f]
137  *
138  * The partial molar Heat Capacity for species *k* is
139  *
140  * \f[
141  * \tilde{Cp}_k(T,P) = Cp^o_k(T,P) = Cp^{ref}_k(T)
142  * \f]
143  *
144  * ## %Application within Kinetics Managers
145  *
146  * \f$ C^a_k\f$ are defined such that \f$ a_k = C^a_k / C^s_k, \f$ where \f$
147  * C^s_k \f$ is a standard concentration defined below and \f$ a_k \f$ are
148  * activities used in the thermodynamic functions. These activity (or
149  * generalized) concentrations are used by kinetics manager classes to compute
150  * the forward and reverse rates of elementary reactions. The activity
151  * concentration,\f$ C^a_k \f$,is given by the following expression.
152  *
153  * \f[
154  * C^a_k = C^s_k X_k = \frac{P}{R T} X_k
155  * \f]
156  *
157  * The standard concentration for species *k* is independent of *k* and equal to
158  *
159  * \f[
160  * C^s_k = C^s = \frac{P}{R T}
161  * \f]
162  *
163  * For example, a bulk-phase binary gas reaction between species j and k,
164  * producing a new gas species l would have the following equation for its rate
165  * of progress variable, \f$ R^1 \f$, which has units of kmol m-3 s-1.
166  *
167  * \f[
168  * R^1 = k^1 C_j^a C_k^a = k^1 (C^s a_j) (C^s a_k)
169  * \f]
170  * where
171  * \f[
172  * C_j^a = C^s a_j \quad \mbox{and} \quad C_k^a = C^s a_k
173  * \f]
174  *
175  * \f$ C_j^a \f$ is the activity concentration of species j, and
176  * \f$ C_k^a \f$ is the activity concentration of species k. \f$ C^s \f$ is the
177  * standard concentration. \f$ a_j \f$ is the activity of species j which is
178  * equal to the mole fraction of j.
179  *
180  * The reverse rate constant can then be obtained from the law of microscopic
181  * reversibility and the equilibrium expression for the system.
182  *
183  * \f[
184  * \frac{a_j a_k}{ a_l} = K_a^{o,1} = \exp(\frac{\mu^o_l - \mu^o_j - \mu^o_k}{R T} )
185  * \f]
186  *
187  * \f$ K_a^{o,1} \f$ is the dimensionless form of the equilibrium constant,
188  * associated with the pressure dependent standard states \f$ \mu^o_l(T,P) \f$
189  * and their associated activities, \f$ a_l \f$, repeated here:
190  *
191  * \f[
192  * \mu_l(T,P) = \mu^o_l(T, P) + R T \log(a_l)
193  * \f]
194  *
195  * We can switch over to expressing the equilibrium constant in terms of the
196  * reference state chemical potentials
197  *
198  * \f[
199  * K_a^{o,1} = \exp(\frac{\mu^{ref}_l - \mu^{ref}_j - \mu^{ref}_k}{R T} ) * \frac{P_{ref}}{P}
200  * \f]
201  *
202  * The concentration equilibrium constant, \f$ K_c \f$, may be obtained by
203  * changing over to activity concentrations. When this is done:
204  *
205  * \f[
206  * \frac{C^a_j C^a_k}{ C^a_l} = C^o K_a^{o,1} = K_c^1 =
207  * \exp(\frac{\mu^{ref}_l - \mu^{ref}_j - \mu^{ref}_k}{R T} ) * \frac{P_{ref}}{RT}
208  * \f]
209  *
210  * %Kinetics managers will calculate the concentration equilibrium constant,
211  * \f$ K_c \f$, using the second and third part of the above expression as a
212  * definition for the concentration equilibrium constant.
213  *
214  * For completeness, the pressure equilibrium constant may be obtained as well
215  *
216  * \f[
217  * \frac{P_j P_k}{ P_l P_{ref}} = K_p^1 =
218  * \exp\left(\frac{\mu^{ref}_l - \mu^{ref}_j - \mu^{ref}_k}{R T} \right)
219  * \f]
220  *
221  * \f$ K_p \f$ is the simplest form of the equilibrium constant for ideal gases.
222  * However, it isn't necessarily the simplest form of the equilibrium constant
223  * for other types of phases; \f$ K_c \f$ is used instead because it is
224  * completely general.
225  *
226  * The reverse rate of progress may be written down as
227  * \f[
228  * R^{-1} = k^{-1} C_l^a = k^{-1} (C^o a_l)
229  * \f]
230  *
231  * where we can use the concept of microscopic reversibility to write the
232  * reverse rate constant in terms of the forward rate constant and the
233  * concentration equilibrium constant, \f$ K_c \f$.
234  *
235  * \f[
236  * k^{-1} = k^1 K^1_c
237  * \f]
238  *
239  * \f$k^{-1} \f$ has units of s-1.
240  *
241  * ## Instantiation of the Class
242  *
243  * The constructor for this phase is located in the default ThermoFactory for
244  * %Cantera. A new IdealGasPhase may be created by the following code snippet:
245  *
246  * @code
247  * XML_Node *xc = get_XML_File("silane.xml");
248  * XML_Node * const xs = xc->findNameID("phase", "silane");
249  * ThermoPhase *silane_tp = newPhase(*xs);
250  * IdealGasPhase *silaneGas = dynamic_cast <IdealGasPhase *>(silane_tp);
251  * @endcode
252  *
253  * or by the following constructor:
254  *
255  * @code
256  * XML_Node *xc = get_XML_File("silane.xml");
257  * XML_Node * const xs = xc->findNameID("phase", "silane");
258  * IdealGasPhase *silaneGas = new IdealGasPhase(*xs);
259  * @endcode
260  *
261  * ## XML Example
262  *
263  * An example of an XML Element named phase setting up a IdealGasPhase
264  * object named silane is given below.
265  *
266  * @code
267  * <!-- phase silane -->
268  * <phase dim="3" id="silane">
269  * <elementArray datasrc="elements.xml"> Si H He </elementArray>
270  * <speciesArray datasrc="#species_data">
271  * H2 H HE SIH4 SI SIH SIH2 SIH3 H3SISIH SI2H6
272  * H2SISIH2 SI3H8 SI2 SI3
273  * </speciesArray>
274  * <reactionArray datasrc="#reaction_data"/>
275  * <thermo model="IdealGas"/>
276  * <kinetics model="GasKinetics"/>
277  * <transport model="None"/>
278  * </phase>
279  * @endcode
280  *
281  * The model attribute "IdealGas" of the thermo XML element identifies the phase
282  * as being of the type handled by the IdealGasPhase object.
283  *
284  * @ingroup thermoprops
285  */

454  /**
455  * @name Chemical Potentials and Activities
456  *
457  * The activity \f$a_k\f$ of a species in solution is
458  * related to the chemical potential by
459  * \f[
460  * \mu_k(T,P,X_k) = \mu_k^0(T,P)
461  * + \hat R T \log a_k.
462  * \f]
463  * The quantity \f$\mu_k^0(T,P)\f$ is the standard state chemical potential
464  * at unit activity. It may depend on the pressure and the temperature.
465  * However, it may not depend on the mole fractions of the species in the
466  * solution.
467  *
468  * The activities are related to the generalized concentrations, \f$\tilde
469  * C_k\f$, and standard concentrations, \f$C^0_k\f$, by the following
470  * formula:
471  *
472  * \f[
473  * a_k = \frac{\tilde C_k}{C^0_k}
474  * \f]
475  * The generalized concentrations are used in the kinetics classes to
476  * describe the rates of progress of reactions involving the species. Their
477  * formulation depends upon the specification of the rate constants for
478  * reaction, especially the units used in specifying the rate constants. The
479  * bridge between the thermodynamic equilibrium expressions that use a_k and
480  * the kinetics expressions which use the generalized concentrations is
481  * provided by the multiplicative factor of the standard concentrations.
482  * @{
483  */
