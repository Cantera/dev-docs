80 /**
81  * Class HMWSoln represents a dilute or concentrated liquid electrolyte
82  * phase which obeys the Pitzer formulation for nonideality.
83  *
84  * As a prerequisite to the specification of thermodynamic quantities,
85  * The concentrations of the ionic species are assumed to obey the
86  * electroneutrality condition.
87  *
88  * <HR>
89  * <H2> Specification of Species Standard State Properties </H2>
90  * <HR>
91  *
92  * The solvent is assumed to be liquid water. A real model for liquid water
93  * (IAPWS 1995 formulation) is used as its standard state. All standard state
94  * properties for the solvent are based on this real model for water, and
95  * involve function calls to the object that handles the real water model,
96  * #Cantera::WaterPropsIAPWS.
97  *
98  * The standard states for solutes are on the unit molality basis. Therefore, in
99  * the documentation below, the normal \f$ o \f$ superscript is replaced with
100  * the \f$ \triangle \f$ symbol. The reference state symbol is now
101  * \f$ \triangle, ref \f$.
102  *
103  * It is assumed that the reference state thermodynamics may be obtained by a
104  * pointer to a populated species thermodynamic property manager class (see
105  * ThermoPhase::m_spthermo). How to relate pressure changes to the reference
106  * state thermodynamics is resolved at this level.
107  *
108  * For solutes that rely on ThermoPhase::m_spthermo, are assumed to have an
109  * incompressible standard state mechanical property. In other words, the molar
110  * volumes are independent of temperature and pressure.
111  *
112  * For these incompressible, standard states, the molar internal energy is
113  * independent of pressure. Since the thermodynamic properties are specified by
114  * giving the standard-state enthalpy, the term \f$ P_0 \hat v\f$ is subtracted
115  * from the specified molar enthalpy to compute the molar internal energy. The
116  * entropy is assumed to be independent of the pressure.
117  *
118  * The enthalpy function is given by the following relation.
119  *
120  * \f[
121  * h^\triangle_k(T,P) = h^{\triangle,ref}_k(T)
122  * + \tilde{v}_k \left( P - P_{ref} \right)
123  * \f]
124  *
125  * For an incompressible, stoichiometric substance, the molar internal energy is
126  * independent of pressure. Since the thermodynamic properties are specified by
127  * giving the standard-state enthalpy, the term \f$ P_{ref} \tilde v\f$ is
128  * subtracted from the specified reference molar enthalpy to compute the molar
129  * internal energy.
130  *
131  * \f[
132  * u^\triangle_k(T,P) = h^{\triangle,ref}_k(T) - P_{ref} \tilde{v}_k
133  * \f]
134  *
135  * The solute standard state heat capacity and entropy are independent of
136  * pressure. The solute standard state Gibbs free energy is obtained from the
137  * enthalpy and entropy functions.
138  *
139  * The vector Phase::m_speciesSize[] is used to hold the base values of species
140  * sizes. These are defined as the molar volumes of species at infinite dilution
141  * at 300 K and 1 atm of water. m_speciesSize are calculated during the
142  * initialization of the HMWSoln object and are then not touched.
143  *
144  * The current model assumes that an incompressible molar volume for all
145  * solutes. The molar volume for the water solvent, however, is obtained from a
146  * pure water equation of state, waterSS. Therefore, the water standard state
147  * varies with both T and P. It is an error to request standard state water
148  * properties at a T and P where the water phase is not a stable phase, i.e.,
149  * beyond its spinodal curve.
150  *
151  * <HR>
152  * <H2> Specification of Solution Thermodynamic Properties </H2>
153  * <HR>
154  *
155  * Chemical potentials of the solutes, \f$ \mu_k \f$, and the solvent, \f$ \mu_o
156  * \f$, which are based on the molality form, have the following general format:
157  *
158  * \f[
159  * \mu_k = \mu^{\triangle}_k(T,P) + R T ln(\gamma_k^{\triangle} \frac{m_k}{m^\triangle})
160  * \f]
161  * \f[
162  * \mu_o = \mu^o_o(T,P) + RT ln(a_o)
163  * \f]
164  *
165  * where \f$ \gamma_k^{\triangle} \f$ is the molality based activity coefficient
166  * for species \f$k\f$.
167  *
168  * Individual activity coefficients of ions can not be independently measured.
169  * Instead, only binary pairs forming electroneutral solutions can be measured.
170  * This problem leads to a redundancy in the evaluation of species standard
171  * state properties. The redundancy issue is resolved by setting the standard
172  * state chemical potential enthalpy, entropy, and volume for the hydrogen ion,
173  * H+, to zero, for every temperature and pressure. After this convention is
174  * applied, all other standard state properties of ionic species contain
175  * meaningful information.
176  *
177  * <H3> Ionic Strength </H3>
178  *
179  * Most of the parameterizations within the model use the ionic strength as a
180  * key variable. The ionic strength, \f$ I\f$ is defined as follows
181  *
182  * \f[
183  * I = \frac{1}{2} \sum_k{m_k z_k^2}
184  * \f]
185  *
186  * \f$ m_k \f$ is the molality of the kth species. \f$ z_k \f$ is the charge of
187  * the kth species. Note, the ionic strength is a defined units quantity. The
188  * molality has defined units of gmol kg-1, and therefore the ionic strength has
189  * units of sqrt( gmol kg<SUP>-1</SUP>).
190  *
191  * In some instances, from some authors, a different formulation is used for the
192  * ionic strength in the equations below. The different formulation is due to
193  * the possibility of the existence of weak acids and how association wrt to the
194  * weak acid equilibrium relation affects the calculation of the activity
195  * coefficients via the assumed value of the ionic strength.
196  *
197  * If we are to assume that the association reaction doesn't have an effect on
198  * the ionic strength, then we will want to consider the associated weak acid as
199  * in effect being fully dissociated, when we calculate an effective value for
200  * the ionic strength. We will call this calculated value, the stoichiometric
201  * ionic strength, \f$ I_s \f$, putting a subscript s to denote it from the more
202  * straightforward calculation of \f$ I \f$.
203  *
204  * \f[
205  * I_s = \frac{1}{2} \sum_k{m_k^s z_k^2}
206  * \f]
207  *
208  * Here, \f$ m_k^s \f$ is the value of the molalities calculated assuming that
209  * all weak acid-base pairs are in their fully dissociated states. This
210  * calculation may be simplified by considering that the weakly associated acid
211  * may be made up of two charged species, k1 and k2, each with their own
212  * charges, obeying the following relationship:
213  *
214  * \f[
215  * z_k = z_{k1} + z_{k2}
216  * \f]
217  * Then, we may only need to specify one charge value, say, \f$ z_{k1}\f$, the
218  * cation charge number, in order to get both numbers, since we have already
219  * specified \f$ z_k \f$ in the definition of original species. Then, the
220  * stoichiometric ionic strength may be calculated via the following formula.
221  *
222  * \f[
223  * I_s = \frac{1}{2} \left(\sum_{k,ions}{m_k z_k^2}+
224  * \sum_{k,weak_assoc}(m_k z_{k1}^2 + m_k z_{k2}^2) \right)
225  * \f]
226  *
227  * The specification of which species are weakly associated acids is made in the
228  * input file via the <TT> stoichIsMods </TT> XML block, where the charge for k1
229  * is also specified. An example is given below:
230  *
231  * @code
232  * <stoichIsMods>
233  * NaCl(aq):-1.0
234  * </stoichIsMods>
235  * @endcode
236  *
237  * Because we need the concept of a weakly associated acid in order to calculated
238  * \f$ I_s \f$ we need to catalog all species in the phase. This is done using
239  * the following categories:
240  *
241  * - <B>cEST_solvent</B> : Solvent species (neutral)
242  * - <B>cEST_chargedSpecies</B> Charged species (charged)
243  * - <B>cEST_weakAcidAssociated</B> Species which can break apart into charged species.
244  * It may or may not be charged. These may or
245  * may not be be included in the
246  * species solution vector.
247  * - <B>cEST_strongAcidAssociated</B> Species which always breaks apart into charged species.
248  * It may or may not be charged. Normally, these
249  * aren't included in the speciation vector.
250  * - <B>cEST_polarNeutral </B> Polar neutral species
251  * - <B>cEST_nonpolarNeutral</B> Non polar neutral species
252  *
253  * Polar and non-polar neutral species are differentiated, because some
254  * additions to the activity coefficient expressions distinguish between these
255  * two types of solutes. This is the so-called salt-out effect.
256  *
257  * The type of species is specified in the <TT>electrolyteSpeciesType</TT> XML
258  * block. Note, this is not considered a part of the specification of the
259  * standard state for the species, at this time. Therefore, this information is
260  * put under the <TT>activityCoefficient</TT> XML block. An example is given
261  * below
262  *
263  * @code
264  * <electrolyteSpeciesType>
265  * H2L(L):solvent
266  * H+:chargedSpecies
267  * NaOH(aq):weakAcidAssociated
268  * NaCl(aq):strongAcidAssociated
269  * NH3(aq):polarNeutral
270  * O2(aq):nonpolarNeutral
271  * </electrolyteSpeciesType>
272  * @endcode
273  *
274  * Much of the species electrolyte type information is inferred from other
275  * information in the input file. For example, as species which is charged is
276  * given the "chargedSpecies" default category. A neutral solute species is put
277  * into the "nonpolarNeutral" category by default.
278  *
279  * <H3> Specification of the Excess Gibbs Free Energy </H3>
280  *
281  * Pitzer's formulation may best be represented as a specification of the excess
282  * Gibbs free energy, \f$ G^{ex} \f$, defined as the deviation of the total
283  * Gibbs free energy from that of an ideal molal solution.
284  * \f[
285  * G = G^{id} + G^{ex}
286  * \f]
287  *
288  * The ideal molal solution contribution, not equal to an ideal solution
289  * contribution and in fact containing a singularity at the zero solvent mole
290  * fraction limit, is given below.
291  * \f[
292  * G^{id} = n_o \mu^o_o + \sum_{k\ne o} n_k \mu_k^{\triangle}
293  * + \tilde{M}_o n_o ( RT (\sum{m_i(\ln(m_i)-1)}))
294  * \f]
295  *
296  * From the excess Gibbs free energy formulation, the activity coefficient
297  * expression and the osmotic coefficient expression for the solvent may be
298  * defined, by taking the appropriate derivatives. Using this approach
299  * guarantees that the entire system will obey the Gibbs-Duhem relations.
300  *
301  * Pitzer employs the following general expression for the excess Gibbs free
302  * energy
303  *
304  * \f[
305  * \begin{array}{cclc}
306  * \frac{G^{ex}}{\tilde{M}_o n_o RT} &= &
307  * \left( \frac{4A_{Debye}I}{3b} \right) \ln(1 + b \sqrt{I})
308  * + 2 \sum_c \sum_a m_c m_a B_{ca}
309  * + \sum_c \sum_a m_c m_a Z C_{ca}
310  * \\&&
311  * + \sum_{c < c'} \sum m_c m_{c'} \left[ 2 \Phi_{c{c'}} + \sum_a m_a \Psi_{c{c'}a} \right]
312  * + \sum_{a < a'} \sum m_a m_{a'} \left[ 2 \Phi_{a{a'}} + \sum_c m_c \Psi_{a{a'}c} \right]
313  * \\&&
314  * + 2 \sum_n \sum_c m_n m_c \lambda_{nc} + 2 \sum_n \sum_a m_n m_a \lambda_{na}
315  * + 2 \sum_{n < n'} \sum m_n m_{n'} \lambda_{n{n'}}
316  * + \sum_n m^2_n \lambda_{nn}
317  * \end{array}
318  * \f]
319  *
320  * <I>a</I> is a subscript over all anions, <I>c</I> is a subscript extending
321  * over all cations, and <I>i</I> is a subscript that extends over all anions
322  * and cations. <I>n</I> is a subscript that extends only over neutral solute
323  * molecules. The second line contains cross terms where cations affect cations
324  * and/or cation/anion pairs, and anions affect anions or cation/anion pairs.
325  * Note part of the coefficients, \f$ \Phi_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi_{a{a'}} \f$
326  * stem from the theory of unsymmetrical mixing of electrolytes with different
327  * charges. This theory depends on the total ionic strength of the solution, and
328  * therefore, \f$ \Phi_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi_{a{a'}} \f$ will depend on
329  * <I>I</I>, the ionic strength. \f$ B_{ca}\f$ is a strong function of the
330  * total ionic strength, <I>I</I>, of the electrolyte. The rest of the
331  * coefficients are assumed to be independent of the molalities or ionic
332  * strengths. However, all coefficients are potentially functions of the
333  * temperature and pressure of the solution.
334  *
335  * <I>A</I> is the Debye-Huckel constant. Its specification is described in its
336  * own section below.
337  *
338  * \f$ I\f$ is the ionic strength of the solution, and is given by:
339  *
340  * \f[
341  * I = \frac{1}{2} \sum_k{m_k z_k^2}
342  * \f]
343  *
344  * In contrast to several other Debye-Huckel implementations (see \ref
345  * DebyeHuckel), the parameter \f$ b\f$ in the above equation is a constant that
346  * does not vary with respect to ion identity. This is an important
347  * simplification as it avoids troubles with satisfaction of the Gibbs-Duhem
348  * analysis.
349  *
350  * The function \f$ Z \f$ is given by
351  *
352  * \f[
353  * Z = \sum_i m_i \left| z_i \right|
354  * \f]
355  *
356  * The value of \f$ B_{ca}\f$ is given by the following function
357  *
358  * \f[
359  * B_{ca} = \beta^{(0)}_{ca} + \beta^{(1)}_{ca} g(\alpha^{(1)}_{ca} \sqrt{I})
360  * + \beta^{(2)}_{ca} g(\alpha^{(2)}_{ca} \sqrt{I})
361  * \f]
362  *
363  * where
364  *
365  * \f[
366  * g(x) = 2 \frac{(1 - (1 + x)\exp[-x])}{x^2}
367  * \f]
368  *
369  * The formulation for \f$ B_{ca}\f$ combined with the formulation of the Debye-
370  * Huckel term in the eqn. for the excess Gibbs free energy stems essentially
371  * from an empirical fit to the ionic strength dependent data based over a wide
372  * sampling of binary electrolyte systems. \f$ C_{ca} \f$, \f$ \lambda_{nc} \f$,
373  * \f$ \lambda_{na} \f$, \f$ \lambda_{nn} \f$, \f$ \Psi_{c{c'}a} \f$, \f$
374  * \Psi_{a{a'}c} \f$ are experimentally derived coefficients that may have
375  * pressure and/or temperature dependencies.
376  *
377  * The \f$ \Phi_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi_{a{a'}} \f$ formulations are slightly
378  * more complicated. \f$ b \f$ is a universal constant defined to be equal to
379  * \f$ 1.2\ kg^{1/2}\ gmol^{-1/2} \f$. The exponential coefficient \f$
380  * \alpha^{(1)}_{ca} \f$ is usually fixed at \f$ \alpha^{(1)}_{ca} = 2.0\
381  * kg^{1/2} gmol^{-1/2}\f$ except for 2-2 electrolytes, while other parameters
382  * were fit to experimental data. For 2-2 electrolytes, \f$ \alpha^{(1)}_{ca} =
383  * 1.4\ kg^{1/2}\ gmol^{-1/2}\f$ is used in combination with either \f$
384  * \alpha^{(2)}_{ca} = 12\ kg^{1/2}\ gmol^{-1/2}\f$ or \f$ \alpha^{(2)}_{ca} = k
385  * A_\psi \f$, where <I>k</I> is a constant. For electrolytes other than 2-2
386  * electrolytes the \f$ \beta^{(2)}_{ca} g(\alpha^{(2)}_{ca} \sqrt{I}) \f$ term
387  * is not used in the fitting procedure; it is only used for divalent metal
388  * solfates and other high-valence electrolytes which exhibit significant
389  * association at low ionic strengths.
390  *
391  * The \f$ \beta^{(0)}_{ca} \f$, \f$ \beta^{(1)}_{ca}\f$, \f$ \beta^{(2)}_{ca}
392  * \f$, and \f$ C_{ca} \f$ binary coefficients are referred to as ion-
393  * interaction or Pitzer parameters. These Pitzer parameters may vary with
394  * temperature and pressure but they do not depend on the ionic strength. Their
395  * values and temperature derivatives of their values have been tabulated for a
396  * range of electrolytes
397  *
398  * The \f$ \Phi_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi_{a{a'}} \f$ contributions, which
399  * capture cation-cation and anion-anion interactions, also have an ionic
400  * strength dependence.
401  *
402  * Ternary contributions \f$ \Psi_{c{c'}a} \f$ and \f$ \Psi_{a{a'}c} \f$ have
403  * been measured also for some systems. The success of the Pitzer method lies in
404  * its ability to model nonlinear activity coefficients of complex
405  * multicomponent systems with just binary and minor ternary contributions,
406  * which can be independently measured in binary or ternary subsystems.
407  *
408  * <H3> Multicomponent Activity Coefficients for Solutes </H3>
409  *
410  * The formulas for activity coefficients of solutes may be obtained by taking
411  * the following derivative of the excess Gibbs Free Energy formulation
412  * described above:
413  *
414  * \f[
415  * \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle) = \frac{d\left( \frac{G^{ex}}{M_o n_o RT} \right)}{d(m_k)}\Bigg|_{n_i}
416  * \f]
417  *
418  * In the formulas below the following conventions are used. The subscript
419  * <I>M</I> refers to a particular cation. The subscript X refers to a
420  * particular anion, whose activity is being currently evaluated. the subscript
421  * <I>a</I> refers to a summation over all anions in the solution, while the
422  * subscript <I>c</I> refers to a summation over all cations in the solutions.
423  *
424  * The activity coefficient for a particular cation <I>M</I> is given by
425  *
426  * \f[
427  * \ln(\gamma_M^\triangle) = -z_M^2(F) + \sum_a m_a \left( 2 B_{Ma} + Z C_{Ma} \right)
428  * + z_M \left( \sum_a \sum_c m_a m_c C_{ca} \right)
429  * + \sum_c m_c \left[ 2 \Phi_{Mc} + \sum_a m_a \Psi_{Mca} \right]
430  * + \sum_{a < a'} \sum m_a m_{a'} \Psi_{Ma{a'}}
431  * + 2 \sum_n m_n \lambda_{nM}
432  * \f]
433  *
434  * The activity coefficient for a particular anion <I>X</I> is given by
435  *
436  * \f[
437  * \ln(\gamma_X^\triangle) = -z_X^2(F) + \sum_a m_c \left( 2 B_{cX} + Z C_{cX} \right)
438  * + \left|z_X \right| \left( \sum_a \sum_c m_a m_c C_{ca} \right)
439  * + \sum_a m_a \left[ 2 \Phi_{Xa} + \sum_c m_c \Psi_{cXa} \right]
440  * + \sum_{c < c'} \sum m_c m_{c'} \Psi_{c{c'}X}
441  * + 2 \sum_n m_n \lambda_{nM}
442  * \f]
443  * where the function \f$ F \f$ is given by
444  *
445  * \f[
446  * F = - A_{\phi} \left[ \frac{\sqrt{I}}{1 + b \sqrt{I}}
447  * + \frac{2}{b} \ln{\left(1 + b\sqrt{I}\right)} \right]
448  * + \sum_a \sum_c m_a m_c B'_{ca}
449  * + \sum_{c < c'} \sum m_c m_{c'} \Phi'_{c{c'}}
450  * + \sum_{a < a'} \sum m_a m_{a'} \Phi'_{a{a'}}
451  * \f]
452  *
453  * We have employed the definition of \f$ A_{\phi} \f$, also used by Pitzer
454  * which is equal to
455  *
456  * \f[
457  * A_{\phi} = \frac{A_{Debye}}{3}
458  * \f]
459  *
460  * In the above formulas, \f$ \Phi'_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi'_{a{a'}} \f$ are the
461  * ionic strength derivatives of \f$ \Phi_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi_{a{a'}} \f$,
462  * respectively.
463  *
464  * The function \f$ B'_{MX} \f$ is defined as:
465  *
466  * \f[
467  * B'_{MX} = \left( \frac{\beta^{(1)}_{MX} h(\alpha^{(1)}_{MX} \sqrt{I})}{I} \right)
468  * \left( \frac{\beta^{(2)}_{MX} h(\alpha^{(2)}_{MX} \sqrt{I})}{I} \right)
469  * \f]
470  *
471  * where \f$ h(x) \f$ is defined as
472  *
473  * \f[
474  * h(x) = g'(x) \frac{x}{2} =
475  * \frac{2\left(1 - \left(1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} \right)\exp(-x) \right)}{x^2}
476  * \f]
477  *
478  * The activity coefficient for neutral species <I>N</I> is given by
479  *
480  * \f[
481  * \ln(\gamma_N^\triangle) = 2 \left( \sum_i m_i \lambda_{iN}\right)
482  * \f]
483  *
484  * <H3> Activity of the Water Solvent </H3>
485  *
486  * The activity for the solvent water,\f$ a_o \f$, is not independent and must
487  * be determined either from the Gibbs-Duhem relation or from taking the
488  * appropriate derivative of the same excess Gibbs free energy function as was
489  * used to formulate the solvent activity coefficients. Pitzer's description
490  * follows the later approach to derive a formula for the osmotic coefficient,
491  * \f$ \phi \f$.
492  *
493  * \f[
494  * \phi - 1 = - \left( \frac{d\left(\frac{G^{ex}}{RT} \right)}{d(\tilde{M}_o n_o)} \right)
495  * \frac{1}{\sum_{i \ne 0} m_i}
496  * \f]
497  *
498  * The osmotic coefficient may be related to the water activity by the following relation:
499  *
500  * \f[
501  * \phi = - \frac{1}{\tilde{M}_o \sum_{i \neq o} m_i} \ln(a_o)
502  * = - \frac{n_o}{\sum_{i \neq o}n_i} \ln(a_o)
503  * \f]
504  *
505  * The result is the following
506  *
507  * \f[
508  * \begin{array}{ccclc}
509  * \phi - 1 &= &
510  * \frac{2}{\sum_{i \ne 0} m_i}
511  * \bigg[ &
512  * - A_{\phi} \frac{I^{3/2}}{1 + b \sqrt{I}}
513  * + \sum_c \sum_a m_c m_a \left( B^{\phi}_{ca} + Z C_{ca}\right)
514  * \\&&&
515  * + \sum_{c < c'} \sum m_c m_{c'} \left[ \Phi^{\phi}_{c{c'}} + \sum_a m_a \Psi_{c{c'}a} \right]
516  * + \sum_{a < a'} \sum m_a m_{a'} \left[ \Phi^{\phi}_{a{a'}} + \sum_c m_c \Psi_{a{a'}c} \right]
517  * \\&&&
518  * + \sum_n \sum_c m_n m_c \lambda_{nc} + \sum_n \sum_a m_n m_a \lambda_{na}
519  * + \sum_{n < n'} \sum m_n m_{n'} \lambda_{n{n'}}
520  * + \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum_n m^2_n \lambda_{nn}\right)
521  * \bigg]
522  * \end{array}
523  * \f]
524  *
525  * It can be shown that the expression
526  *
527  * \f[
528  * B^{\phi}_{ca} = \beta^{(0)}_{ca} + \beta^{(1)}_{ca} \exp{(- \alpha^{(1)}_{ca} \sqrt{I})}
529  * + \beta^{(2)}_{ca} \exp{(- \alpha^{(2)}_{ca} \sqrt{I} )}
530  * \f]
531  *
532  * is consistent with the expression \f$ B_{ca} \f$ in the \f$ G^{ex} \f$
533  * expression after carrying out the derivative wrt \f$ m_M \f$.
534  *
535  * Also taking into account that \f$ {\Phi}_{c{c'}} \f$ and
536  * \f$ {\Phi}_{a{a'}} \f$ has an ionic strength dependence.
537  *
538  * \f[
539  * \Phi^{\phi}_{c{c'}} = {\Phi}_{c{c'}} + I \frac{d{\Phi}_{c{c'}}}{dI}
540  * \f]
541  *
542  * \f[
543  * \Phi^{\phi}_{a{a'}} = \Phi_{a{a'}} + I \frac{d\Phi_{a{a'}}}{dI}
544  * \f]
545  *
546  * <H3> Temperature and Pressure Dependence of the Pitzer Parameters </H3>
547  *
548  * In general most of the coefficients introduced in the previous section may
549  * have a temperature and pressure dependence. The temperature and pressure
550  * dependence of these coefficients strongly influence the value of the excess
551  * Enthalpy and excess Volumes of Pitzer solutions. Therefore, these are readily
552  * measurable quantities. HMWSoln provides several different methods for putting
553  * these dependencies into the coefficients. HMWSoln has an implementation
554  * described by Silverter and Pitzer (1977), which was used to fit experimental
555  * data for NaCl over an extensive range, below the critical temperature of
556  * water. They found a temperature functional form for fitting the 3 following
557  * coefficients that describe the Pitzer parameterization for a single salt to
558  * be adequate to describe how the excess Gibbs free energy values for the
559  * binary salt changes with respect to temperature. The following functional
560  * form was used to fit the temperature dependence of the Pitzer Coefficients
561  * for each cation - anion pair, M X.
562  *
563  * \f[
564  * \beta^{(0)}_{MX} = q^{b0}_0
565  * + q^{b0}_1 \left( T - T_r \right)
566  * + q^{b0}_2 \left( T^2 - T_r^2 \right)
567  * + q^{b0}_3 \left( \frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_r}\right)
568  * + q^{b0}_4 \ln \left( \frac{T}{T_r} \right)
569  * \f]
570  * \f[
571  * \beta^{(1)}_{MX} = q^{b1}_0 + q^{b1}_1 \left( T - T_r \right)
572  * + q^{b1}_{2} \left( T^2 - T_r^2 \right)
573  * \f]
574  * \f[
575  * C^{\phi}_{MX} = q^{Cphi}_0
576  * + q^{Cphi}_1 \left( T - T_r \right)
577  * + q^{Cphi}_2 \left( T^2 - T_r^2 \right)
578  * + q^{Cphi}_3 \left( \frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_r}\right)
579  * + q^{Cphi}_4 \ln \left( \frac{T}{T_r} \right)
580  * \f]
581  *
582  * where
583  *
584  * \f[
585  * C^{\phi}_{MX} = 2 {\left| z_M z_X \right|}^{1/2} C_{MX}
586  * \f]
587  *
588  * In later papers, Pitzer has added additional temperature dependencies to all
589  * of the other remaining second and third order virial coefficients. Some of
590  * these dependencies are justified and motivated by theory. Therefore, a
591  * formalism wherein all of the coefficients in the base theory have temperature
592  * dependencies associated with them has been implemented within the HMWSoln
593  * object. Much of the formalism, however, has been unexercised.
594  *
595  * In the HMWSoln object, the temperature dependence of the Pitzer parameters
596  * are specified in the following way.
597  *
598  * - PIZTER_TEMP_CONSTANT - string name "CONSTANT"
599  * - Assumes that all coefficients are independent of temperature
600  * and pressure
601  * - PIZTER_TEMP_COMPLEX1 - string name "COMPLEX" or "COMPLEX1"
602  * - Uses the full temperature dependence for the
603  * \f$\beta^{(0)}_{MX} \f$ (5 coeffs),
604  * the \f$\beta^{(1)}_{MX} \f$ (3 coeffs),
605  * and \f$ C^{\phi}_{MX} \f$ (5 coeffs) parameters described above.
606  * - PITZER_TEMP_LINEAR - string name "LINEAR"
607  * - Uses just the temperature dependence for the
608  * \f$\beta^{(0)}_{MX} \f$, the \f$\beta^{(1)}_{MX} \f$,
609  * and \f$ C^{\phi}_{MX} \f$ coefficients described above.
610  * There are 2 coefficients for each term.
611  *
612  * The temperature dependence is specified in an attributes field in the <TT>
613  * activityCoefficients </TT> XML block, called <TT> TempModel </TT>.
614  * Permissible values for that attribute are <TT> CONSTANT, COMPLEX1</TT>, and
615  * <TT> LINEAR.</TT>
616  *
617  * The specification of the binary interaction between a cation and an anion is
618  * given by the coefficients, \f$ B_{MX}\f$ and \f$ C_{MX}\f$ The specification
619  * of \f$ B_{MX}\f$ is a function of \f$\beta^{(0)}_{MX} \f$,
620  * \f$\beta^{(1)}_{MX} \f$, \f$\beta^{(2)}_{MX} \f$, \f$\alpha^{(1)}_{MX} \f$,
621  * and \f$\alpha^{(2)}_{MX} \f$. \f$ C_{MX}\f$ is calculated from
622  * \f$C^{\phi}_{MX} \f$ from the formula above. All of the underlying
623  * coefficients are specified in the XML element block <TT> binarySaltParameters
624  * </TT>, which has the attribute <TT> cation </TT> and <TT> anion </TT> to
625  * identify the interaction. XML elements named <TT> beta0, beta1, beta2, Cphi,
626  * Alpha1, Alpha2 </TT> within each <TT> binarySaltParameters </TT> block
627  * specify the parameters. Within each of these blocks multiple parameters
628  * describing temperature or pressure dependence are serially listed in the
629  * order that they appear in the equation in this document. An example of the
630  * <TT> beta0 </TT> block that fits the <TT> COMPLEX1 </TT> temperature
631  * dependence given above is
632  *
633  * @code
634  * <binarySaltParameters cation="Na+" anion="OH-">
635  * <beta0> q0, q1, q2, q3, q4 </beta0>
636  * </binarySaltParameters>
637  * @endcode
638  *
639  * The parameters for \f$ \beta^{(0)}\f$ fit the following equation:
640  *
641  * \f[
642  * \beta^{(0)} = q_0^{{\beta}0} + q_1^{{\beta}0} \left( T - T_r \right)
643  * + q_2^{{\beta}0} \left( T^2 - T_r^2 \right)
644  * + q_3^{{\beta}0} \left( \frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_r} \right)
645  * + q_4^{{\beta}0} \ln \left( \frac{T}{T_r} \right)
646  * \f]
647  *
648  * This same <TT> COMPLEX1 </TT> temperature
649  * dependence given above is used for the following parameters:
650  * \f$ \beta^{(0)}_{MX} \f$, \f$ \beta^{(1)}_{MX} \f$,
651  * \f$ \beta^{(2)}_{MX} \f$, \f$ \Theta_{cc'} \f$, \f$\Theta_{aa'} \f$,
652  * \f$ \Psi_{c{c'}a} \f$ and \f$ \Psi_{ca{a'}} \f$.
653  *
654  * <H3> Like-Charged Binary Ion Parameters and the Mixing Parameters </H3>
655  *
656  * The previous section contained the functions, \f$ \Phi_{c{c'}} \f$,
657  * \f$ \Phi_{a{a'}} \f$ and their derivatives wrt the ionic strength, \f$
658  * \Phi'_{c{c'}} \f$ and \f$ \Phi'_{a{a'}} \f$. Part of these terms come from
659  * theory.
660  *
661  * Since like charged ions repel each other and are generally not near each
662  * other, the virial coefficients for same-charged ions are small. However,
663  * Pitzer doesn't ignore these in his formulation. Relatively larger and longer
664  * range terms between like-charged ions exist however, which appear only for
665  * unsymmetrical mixing of same-sign charged ions with different charges. \f$
666  * \Phi_{ij} \f$, where \f$ ij \f$ is either \f$ a{a'} \f$ or \f$ c{c'} \f$ is
667  * given by
668  *
669  * \f[
670  * {\Phi}_{ij} = \Theta_{ij} + \,^E \Theta_{ij}(I)
671  * \f]
672  *
673  * \f$ \Theta_{ij} \f$ is the small virial coefficient expansion term. Dependent
674  * in general on temperature and pressure, its ionic strength dependence is
675  * ignored in Pitzer's approach. \f$ \,^E\Theta_{ij}(I) \f$ accounts for the
676  * electrostatic unsymmetrical mixing effects and is dependent only on the
677  * charges of the ions i, j, the total ionic strength and on the dielectric
678  * constant and density of the solvent. This seems to be a relatively well-
679  * documented part of the theory. They theory below comes from Pitzer summation
680  * (Pitzer) in the appendix. It's also mentioned in Bethke's book (Bethke), and
681  * the equations are summarized in Harvie & Weare (1980). Within the code, \f$
682  * \,^E\Theta_{ij}(I) \f$ is evaluated according to the algorithm described in
683  * Appendix B [Pitzer] as
684  *
685  * \f[
686  * \,^E\Theta_{ij}(I) = \left( \frac{z_i z_j}{4I} \right)
687  * \left( J(x_{ij}) - \frac{1}{2} J(x_{ii})
688  * - \frac{1}{2} J(x_{jj}) \right)
689  * \f]
690  *
691  * where \f$ x_{ij} = 6 z_i z_j A_{\phi} \sqrt{I} \f$ and
692  *
693  * \f[
694  * J(x) = \frac{1}{x} \int_0^{\infty}{\left( 1 + q +
695  * \frac{1}{2} q^2 - e^q \right) y^2 dy}
696  * \f]
697  *
698  * and \f$ q = - (\frac{x}{y}) e^{-y} \f$. \f$ J(x) \f$ is evaluated by
699  * numerical integration.
700  *
701  * The \f$ \Theta_{ij} \f$ term is a constant that is specified by the XML
702  * element <TT> thetaCation </TT> and <TT> thetaAnion </TT>, which has the
703  * attribute <TT> cation1 </TT>, <TT> cation2 </TT> and <TT> anion1 </TT>, <TT>
704  * anion2 </TT> respectively to identify the interaction. No temperature or
705  * pressure dependence of this parameter is currently allowed. An example of the
706  * block is presented below.
707  *
708  * @code
709  * <thetaCation cation1="Na+" cation2="H+">
710  * <Theta> 0.036 </Theta>
711  * </thetaCation>
712  * @endcode
713  *

826  * <H3> Specification of the Debye-Huckel Constant </H3>
827  *
828  * In the equations above, the formula for \f$ A_{Debye} \f$ is needed. The
829  * HMWSoln object uses two methods for specifying these quantities. The default
830  * method is to assume that \f$ A_{Debye} \f$ is a constant, given in the
831  * initialization process, and stored in the member double, m_A_Debye.
832  * Optionally, a full water treatment may be employed that makes
833  * \f$ A_{Debye} \f$ a full function of <I>T</I> and <I>P</I> and creates
834  * nontrivial entries for the excess heat capacity, enthalpy, and excess volumes
835  * of solution.
836  *
837  * \f[
838  * A_{Debye} = \frac{F e B_{Debye}}{8 \pi \epsilon R T} {\left( C_o \tilde{M}_o \right)}^{1/2}
839  * \f]
840  * where
841  *
842  * \f[
843  * B_{Debye} = \frac{F} {{(\frac{\epsilon R T}{2})}^{1/2}}
844  * \f]
845  * Therefore:
846  * \f[
847  * A_{Debye} = \frac{1}{8 \pi}
848  * {\left(\frac{2 N_a \rho_o}{1000}\right)}^{1/2}
849  * {\left(\frac{N_a e^2}{\epsilon R T }\right)}^{3/2}
850  * \f]
851  *
852  * Units = sqrt(kg/gmol)
853  *
854  * where
855  * - \f$ N_a \f$ is Avogadro's number
856  * - \f$ \rho_w \f$ is the density of water
857  * - \f$ e \f$ is the electronic charge
858  * - \f$ \epsilon = K \epsilon_o \f$ is the permittivity of water
859  * - \f$ K \f$ is the dielectric constant of water,
860  * - \f$ \epsilon_o \f$ is the permittivity of free space.
861  * - \f$ \rho_o \f$ is the density of the solvent in its standard state.
862  *
863  * Nominal value at 298 K and 1 atm = 1.172576 (kg/gmol)<SUP>1/2</SUP>
864  * based on:
865  * - \f$ \epsilon / \epsilon_0 \f$ = 78.54 (water at 25C)
866  * - T = 298.15 K
867  * - B_Debye = 3.28640E9 (kg/gmol)<SUP>1/2</SUP> m<SUP>-1</SUP>
868  *
869  * An example of a fixed value implementation is given below.
870  * @code
871  * <activityCoefficients model="Pitzer">
872  * <!-- A_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol) -->
873  * <A_Debye> 1.172576 </A_Debye>
874  * <!-- object description continues -->
875  * </activityCoefficients>
876  * @endcode
877  *
878  * An example of a variable value implementation within the HMWSoln object is
879  * given below. The model attribute, "water", triggers the full implementation.
880  *
881  * @code
882  * <activityCoefficients model="Pitzer">
883  * <!-- A_Debye units = sqrt(kg/gmol) -->
884  * <A_Debye model="water" />
885  * <!-- object description continues -->
886  * </activityCoefficients>
887  * @endcode

889  * <H3> Temperature and Pressure Dependence of the Activity Coefficients </H3>
890  *
891  * Temperature dependence of the activity coefficients leads to nonzero terms
892  * for the excess enthalpy and entropy of solution. This means that the partial
893  * molar enthalpies, entropies, and heat capacities are all non-trivial to
894  * compute. The following formulas are used.
895  *
896  * The partial molar enthalpy, \f$ \bar s_k(T,P) \f$:
897  *
898  * \f[
899  * \bar h_k(T,P) = h^{\triangle}_k(T,P)
900  * - R T^2 \frac{d \ln(\gamma_k^\triangle)}{dT}
901  * \f]
902  * The solvent partial molar enthalpy is equal to
903  * \f[
904  * \bar h_o(T,P) = h^{o}_o(T,P) - R T^2 \frac{d \ln(a_o)}{dT}
905  * = h^{o}_o(T,P)
906  * + R T^2 (\sum_{k \neq o} m_k) \tilde{M_o} (\frac{d \phi}{dT})
907  * \f]
908  *
909  * The partial molar entropy, \f$ \bar s_k(T,P) \f$:
910  *
911  * \f[
912  * \bar s_k(T,P) = s^{\triangle}_k(T,P)
913  * - R \ln( \gamma^{\triangle}_k \frac{m_k}{m^{\triangle}}))
914  * - R T \frac{d \ln(\gamma^{\triangle}_k) }{dT}
915  * \f]
916  * \f[
917  * \bar s_o(T,P) = s^o_o(T,P) - R \ln(a_o)
918  * - R T \frac{d \ln(a_o)}{dT}
919  * \f]
920  *
921  * The partial molar heat capacity, \f$ C_{p,k}(T,P)\f$:
922  *
923  * \f[
924  * \bar C_{p,k}(T,P) = C^{\triangle}_{p,k}(T,P)
925  * - 2 R T \frac{d \ln( \gamma^{\triangle}_k)}{dT}
926  * - R T^2 \frac{d^2 \ln(\gamma^{\triangle}_k) }{{dT}^2}
927  * \f]
928  * \f[
929  * \bar C_{p,o}(T,P) = C^o_{p,o}(T,P)
930  * - 2 R T \frac{d \ln(a_o)}{dT}
931  * - R T^2 \frac{d^2 \ln(a_o)}{{dT}^2}
932  * \f]
933  *
934  * The pressure dependence of the activity coefficients leads to non-zero terms
935  * for the excess Volume of the solution. Therefore, the partial molar volumes
936  * are functions of the pressure derivatives of the activity coefficients.
937  * \f[
938  * \bar V_k(T,P) = V^{\triangle}_k(T,P)
939  * + R T \frac{d \ln(\gamma^{\triangle}_k) }{dP}
940  * \f]
941  * \f[
942  * \bar V_o(T,P) = V^o_o(T,P)
943  * + R T \frac{d \ln(a_o)}{dP}
944  * \f]
945  *
946  * The majority of work for these functions take place in the internal routines
947  * that calculate the first and second derivatives of the log of the activity
948  * coefficients wrt temperature, s_update_dlnMolalityActCoeff_dT(),
949  * s_update_d2lnMolalityActCoeff_dT2(), and the first derivative of the log
950  * activity coefficients wrt pressure, s_update_dlnMolalityActCoeff_dP().
951  *
952  * <HR>
953  * <H2> %Application within Kinetics Managers </H2>
954  * <HR>
955  *
956  * For the time being, we have set the standard concentration for all solute
957  * species in this phase equal to the default concentration of the solvent at
958  * the system temperature and pressure multiplied by Mnaught (kg solvent / gmol
959  * solvent). The solvent standard concentration is just equal to its standard
960  * state concentration.
961  *
962  * This means that the kinetics operator essentially works on an generalized
963  * concentration basis (kmol / m3), with units for the kinetic rate constant
964  * specified as if all reactants (solvent or solute) are on a concentration
965  * basis (kmol /m3). The concentration will be modified by the activity
966  * coefficients.
967  *
968  * For example, a bulk-phase binary reaction between liquid solute species
969  * <I>j</I> and <I>k</I>, producing a new liquid solute species <I>l</I> would
970  * have the following equation for its rate of progress variable, \f$ R^1 \f$,
971  * which has units of kmol m-3 s-1.
972  *
973  * \f[
974  * R^1 = k^1 C_j^a C_k^a = k^1 (C^o_o \tilde{M}_o a_j) (C^o_o \tilde{M}_o a_k)
975  * \f]
976  *
977  * where
978  *
979  * \f[
980  * C_j^a = C^o_o \tilde{M}_o a_j \quad and \quad C_k^a = C^o_o \tilde{M}_o a_k
981  * \f]
982  *
983  * \f$ C_j^a \f$ is the activity concentration of species <I>j</I>, and
984  * \f$ C_k^a \f$ is the activity concentration of species <I>k</I>. \f$ C^o_o \f$
985  * is the concentration of water at 298 K and 1 atm. \f$ \tilde{M}_o \f$ has
986  * units of kg solvent per gmol solvent and is equal to
987  *
988  * \f[
989  * \tilde{M}_o = \frac{M_o}{1000}
990  * \f]
991  *
992  * \f$ a_j \f$ is the activity of species <I>j</I> at the current temperature
993  * and pressure and concentration of the liquid phase is given by the molality
994  * based activity coefficient multiplied by the molality of the jth species.
995  *
996  * \f[
997  * a_j = \gamma_j^\triangle m_j = \gamma_j^\triangle \frac{n_j}{\tilde{M}_o n_o}
998  * \f]
999  *
1000  * \f$k^1 \f$ has units of m<SUP>3</SUP> kmol<SUP>-1</SUP> s<SUP>-1</SUP>.
1001  *
1002  * Therefore the generalized activity concentration of a solute species has the following form
1003  *
1004  * \f[
1005  * C_j^a = C^o_o \frac{\gamma_j^\triangle n_j}{n_o}
1006  * \f]
1007  *
1008  * The generalized activity concentration of the solvent has the same units, but it's a simpler form
1009  *
1010  * \f[
1011  * C_o^a = C^o_o a_o
1012  * \f]
1013  *
1014  * The reverse rate constant can then be obtained from the law of microscopic reversibility
1015  * and the equilibrium expression for the system.
1016  *
1017  * \f[
1018  * \frac{a_j a_k}{ a_l} = K^{o,1} = \exp(\frac{\mu^o_l - \mu^o_j - \mu^o_k}{R T} )
1019  * \f]
1020  *
1021  * \f$ K^{o,1} \f$ is the dimensionless form of the equilibrium constant.
1022  *
1023  * \f[
1024  * R^{-1} = k^{-1} C_l^a = k^{-1} (C_o \tilde{M}_o a_l)
1025  * \f]
1026  *
1027  * where
1028  *
1029  * \f[
1030  * k^{-1} = k^1 K^{o,1} C_o \tilde{M}_o
1031  * \f]
1032  *
1033  * \f$ k^{-1} \f$ has units of s<SUP>-1</SUP>.
1034  *
1035  * Note, this treatment may be modified in the future, as events dictate.
